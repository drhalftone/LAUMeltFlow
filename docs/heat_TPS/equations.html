Heat-shield model — governing equations

Reference solver (the physics) and the GNN surrogate (the learned stand-in), side by side. The whole point: the surrogate learns to imitate the solver's per-cell conduction update, eq. (2).

Part 1 — Reference solver (physics)

1. Heat conduction (the governing PDE)

$$\rho\, c_p \,\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(k\,\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right)$$

Heat stored in a cell (left) = net heat conducted in across its faces (right). Variables:

  • \(T\) = temperature [K], the thing we solve for; it varies with position and time.
  • \(t\) = time [s]; \(x\) = position through the slab [m].
  • \(\rho\) = density [kg/m³] (how much solid mass per unit volume in the cell).
  • \(c_p\) = specific heat capacity [J/(kg·K)] (energy to raise 1 kg by 1 K).
  • \(k\) = thermal conductivity [W/(m·K)] (how readily heat flows through the material).
  • \(\partial T/\partial t\) = rate of temperature change in time; \(\partial T/\partial x\) = temperature gradient in space (slope of \(T\) vs \(x\)).

2. Conduction discretized (what each cell computes)

The slab is split into cells; cell \(i\) has one temperature \(T_i\). "Discretized" just means we replace the continuous derivatives in eq. (1) with differences between neighboring cells. Heat flux across the face between cells \(i\) and \(i{+}1\) (the half-index \(i{+}1/2\) denotes that shared face):

$$q_{i+1/2} = -\,k_{\text{face}}\,\frac{T_{i+1}-T_i}{\Delta x}$$
  • \(q\) = heat flux [W/m²] = how fast heat crosses a face. The minus sign means heat flows from hot to cold (down the gradient).
  • \(i\) = cell index; \(T_{i+1}-T_i\) = temperature difference to the right neighbor.
  • \(\Delta x\) = cell spacing [m] (distance between cell centers).
  • \(k_{\text{face}}\) = conductivity evaluated at the face between the two cells.

Each cell then updates from the imbalance of its two face fluxes (what comes in the left face minus what leaves the right face):

$$T_i^{\,\text{new}} = T_i + \frac{\Delta t}{\rho\, c_p\, \Delta x}\,\big(q_{i-1/2}-q_{i+1/2}\big)$$

\(\Delta t\) = the time-step size [s] (how far forward in time one update moves); \(T_i^{\,\text{new}}\) = the cell's temperature after the step. The driving term is the \(\Delta T/\Delta x\) flux: neighbor temperature difference over the spacing. This single equation is what the GNN learns to imitate.

3. Stable time step (CFL) — the mesh-resolution coupling

CFL = Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy condition: a stability rule for explicit (step-by-step) time marching. If the time step is too large for the cell size, the numerical solution blows up. CFL sets the largest \(\Delta t\) that stays stable:

$$\Delta t = \tfrac{1}{2}\, \frac{\Delta x_{\max}^{2}\,\rho\, c_p}{k\cdot \text{CFL}} \;\propto\; \Delta x^{2} \;\propto\; \frac{1}{N^{2}}$$
  • \(\Delta x_{\max}\) = the largest cell width in the mesh [m].
  • \(\text{CFL}\) = a chosen safety number (\(\geq 1\)); larger = smaller, safer steps.
  • \(N\) = number of cells in the mesh (NBRN). Finer mesh = larger \(N\) = smaller \(\Delta x\).
  • \(\propto\) means "proportional to": \(\Delta t\) scales as \(\Delta x^2\), and since \(\Delta x = L/N\), that is \(1/N^2\).

The \(\Delta x^2\) is exactly why a finer mesh needs a smaller step, and why the snapshot \(\Delta t\) drifted with resolution.

4. Pyrolysis (Arrhenius decomposition)

Pyrolysis = heat-driven chemical breakdown of the material. The solid is a mix of reactive components ("species"); each one decays as it heats, losing mass (which is why \(\rho\) drops as a cell chars). Arrhenius is the standard temperature-dependent reaction-rate law. Each species density \(W_k\) (these are the \(\rho_i\) in the node features) decays at the rate:

$$\frac{dW_k}{dt} = -\,A_k\,W_k^{\psi_k}\,e^{-E_k/T}$$
  • \(W_k\) = density of species \(k\) [kg/m³]; \(dW_k/dt\) = its rate of decay.
  • \(k\) here = species index (1, 2, 3…), not the conductivity \(k\) from above.
  • \(A_k\) = pre-exponential factor [1/s] (sets the overall reaction speed).
  • \(E_k\) = activation energy [K, here folded with the gas constant] (the energy barrier).
  • \(\psi_k\) = reaction order (a dimensionless exponent shaping the rate).
  • \(e^{-E/T}\) = the Arrhenius switch: near zero when \(T\) is low, rising sharply once \(T\) is high — so a cell only pyrolyzes once it gets hot enough.

Solved in closed form over one step (\(W\) = the value at the start of the step, \(W^{\text{new}}\) = after it):

$$W^{\text{new}} = W\,e^{-A\,\Delta t\,e^{-E/T}} \qquad (\psi = 1)$$

5. Derived state (how \(\rho\) maps to the front variables)

$$\rho = \sum_k W_k \qquad \beta = \frac{\rho_v-\rho}{\rho_v-\rho_c} \qquad \text{porosity} = \phi + (\phi_c-\phi)\,\beta$$
  • \(\sum_k W_k\) = sum over all species: the bulk density \(\rho\) is just the total of the per-species densities.
  • \(\rho_v\) = virgin (un-pyrolyzed) bulk density [kg/m³]; \(\rho_c\) = fully-charred bulk density. (In aw1: \(\rho_v \approx 280\), \(\rho_c \approx 220\).)
  • \(\beta\) = char fraction, 0 to 1: \(\beta = 0\) when the cell is fully virgin (\(\rho=\rho_v\)), \(\beta = 1\) when fully char (\(\rho=\rho_c\)). These are the colors in the front figure.
  • porosity = void fraction of the cell (empty pore space, 0 to 1). \(\phi\) = virgin porosity, \(\phi_c\) = char porosity; porosity blends between them by \(\beta\), so it rises as the cell chars (and is the pore network the gas flows through in aw2).

Because \(\rho\) and porosity both follow from the \(\rho_i\), the model predicts only the independent \(\rho_i\) and derives the rest.

(aw2 only — gas transport adds Darcy's law \(u = -\tfrac{K}{\mu}\,\partial p/\partial x\) plus advection. \(u\) = gas velocity, \(K\) = permeability, \(\mu\) = gas viscosity, \(\partial p/\partial x\) = pressure gradient. Skip until aw2.)

Part 2 — GNN surrogate (the learned model)

A learned stand-in for eq. (2). GNN = graph neural network: the mesh is treated as a graph (cells = nodes, shared faces = edges), and small neural networks compute each cell's update from its neighbors. MLP = multi-layer perceptron = a basic feed-forward neural network (a stack of learned linear maps with nonlinearities); here each \(f\) and \(g\) below is one MLP whose weights are learned during training. Notation matches gnn_model.py.

1. Encode node and edges

Node feature \(x_i = [\,T,\ \rho,\ \rho_i,\ \text{porosity}\,]\) — the state vector each cell carries (note: here \(x_i\) is the feature vector of node \(i\), not a position). Edges are relative (neighbor minus self, plus the spacing):

$$e_{i\to j} = \big[\,x_j - x_i,\ \ \Delta x\,\big]$$
  • \(e_{i\to j}\) = the edge feature from cell \(i\) to its neighbor \(j\).
  • \(x_j - x_i\) = how the neighbor's state differs from this cell's (e.g. \(\Delta T\), \(\Delta\rho\)) — the same neighbor-difference the physics flux uses.
  • \(\Delta x\) = the spacing to that neighbor (the geometry input; the reason the model can adapt to different mesh resolutions).

Each is passed through a small MLP "encoder" that maps raw features into a hidden vector:

$$h_i = f_{\text{node}}(x_i), \qquad m_{i\to j} = f_{\text{edge}}(e_{i\to j})$$
  • \(h_i\) = the node's hidden (encoded) state; \(m_{i\to j}\) = the encoded "message" coming along an edge.
  • \(f_{\text{node}},\ f_{\text{edge}}\) = the two encoder MLPs.

2. Message passing (one hop, K = 1)

"Message passing" = each node updates itself by combining its own hidden state with the messages from its neighbors:

$$h_i^{\,\text{new}} = g\big(\,[\,h_i,\ m_{\text{left}},\ m_{\text{right}}\,]\,\big)$$
  • \(m_{\text{left}},\ m_{\text{right}}\) = the encoded messages from the left and right neighbor cells.
  • \([\,\cdot,\ \cdot,\ \cdot\,]\) = concatenation (stack the three vectors into one); \(g\) = the node-update MLP.
  • \(K\) = number of message-passing rounds. \(K = 1\) (one round) = each cell only sees its immediate neighbors, which matches the conduction stencil (heat only flows between touching cells). \(K = 2\) would let each cell reach two cells away (it overfits here).

3. Predict the per-cell delta, then step

$$\Delta x_i = f_{\text{out}}(h_i) \qquad x_i^{\,\text{new}} = x_i + \Delta x_i$$
  • \(f_{\text{out}}\) = the output MLP ("decoder"); \(\Delta x_i\) = the predicted change in the cell's state over one step.
  • The model predicts the change in \([\,T,\ \rho_i\,]\) only; \(\rho\) and porosity are derived from the new \(\rho_i\) via eq. (5).
  • \(x_i^{\,\text{new}} = x_i + \Delta x_i\) = add the change to get the next state, then repeat — "rolling forward" in time, exactly like the solver rolls eq. (2).

4. Training losses (why it works)

A loss \(\mathcal{L}\) is the error the model minimizes during training (smaller = closer to the true answer). \(\|\cdot\|^2\) = squared difference (mean-squared error); "pred" = model prediction, "true" = the solver's answer.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{1-step}} = \big\|\,\Delta x_i^{\text{pred}} - \Delta x_i^{\text{true}}\,\big\|^2$$

One-step loss: match the next-step change. Accurate per step, but small errors accumulate when you chain many steps.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{rollout}} = \sum_{m=1}^{M}\big\|\,x_i^{(m)\,\text{pred}} - x_i^{(m)\,\text{true}}\,\big\|^2 \qquad (M = 4)$$
  • \(m\) = step number within a short rollout; \(M\) = how many steps you unroll (here 4); \(x_i^{(m)}\) = the state after \(m\) steps.
  • This penalizes the whole multi-step trajectory, so the model learns to correct its own accumulating error. This was the fix that made the surrogate usable.
