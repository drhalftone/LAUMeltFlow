## Abstract

Thermal protection system (TPS) design requires repeated evaluation of an expensive,
tightly coupled physics solver that resolves heat conduction, pyrolysis, pyrolysis-gas
transport, and aerothermal surface chemistry. The cost of these evaluations bottlenecks
design-space exploration and optimization. We present a graph neural network (GNN)
surrogate that learns the per-step evolution of a 1D heat-shield pyrolysis solver,
enabling design loops to run orders of magnitude faster than the reference physics code.
As a foundation, we re-implement a trusted Fortran reference solver (a finite-volume
conduction–pyrolysis–Darcy-flow model with an aerothermal boundary condition) in modular,
vectorized Python, and verify it against the original through column-level comparison of
its thermocouple and density outputs, matching boundary quantities to machine precision.
We then train the surrogate on data generated by this validated solver, representing the
mesh as a graph with per-cell state features `[T, ρ, ρᵢ, porosity]` and physically
motivated edge features. On the gas-off `aw1` case, the surrogate reproduces a held-out
heating scenario over a full 60 s rollout to a mean error of 7.6 K (~0.5–1% of the
298–1500 K field) and tracks the pyrolysis-front depth to within a fraction of a
millimeter. This work extends the LAUMeltFlow GNN-surrogate program from canonical flux
problems to a multi-physics engineering solver, and points toward fast, mesh-flexible
surrogates for TPS design.

*Current numbers (real): port validation — aw1 bit-exact (~1e-8), aw2_tc21 to 0.064 K.
Surrogate — 7.6 K mean rollout on aw1. Wall time — Fortran ~3 min vs Python ~15.6 min
for aw2_tc21. `[future]`: surrogate wall-time speedup vs solver (not yet measured);
cross-resolution generalization (in progress, written as Future Work); aw2 surrogate.*

## 3. Methodology
- 3.1 **Python port**
  - Modular re-architecture (one module per physics piece) vs. the monolithic Fortran
  - Vectorization for performance; correctness preserved (bit-identical helper verification)
- 3.2 **Verification approach**
  - Column-level diffs against the Fortran `con.out` (max-abs, mean-abs, max-rel per output)
  - Boundary bit-exactness vs. interior drift; how discrepancies were diagnosed (e.g., FP-ordering)
- 3.3 **Training-data generation**
  - State-space vs. trajectory sampling (the lesson carried over from the meltflow work)
  - Parameter ranges sampled (flux histories, mesh resolution, material params)
- 3.4 **GNN surrogate architecture**
  - Per-cell nodes: features `[T, ρ, ρᵢ, porosity, …]`
  - Edge features: interface attributes (conductance, geometry)
  - Message passing depth (K = 1 or 2); one-step prediction target
  - Training setup: loss, normalization, optimizer

## 4. Results
- 4.1 Port validation (per case: `aw1`, `aw2_tc21`, `aw2_tc22`) — **DONE**
  - aw1: **bit-exact** (all 15 columns to ~1e-8, machine precision)
  - aw2_tc21: **to 0.064 K** (max temp err; mean ~4e-4 K; hot surface 0.06 K; densities ~1e-3 to 1e-9)
  - aw2_tc22: byte-identical config to tc21 (no independent coverage) — note for Martin
  - Verification caution: the "interior drift"/"+37.7 K back-face" were thermocouple-SAMPLING artifacts, not numerical error
  - Fortran-vs-Python validation tables (from `validate.py`)
- 4.2 Surrogate accuracy (aw1, gas off) — **DONE**
  - **7.6 K mean rollout error** over 60 s held-out scenario (~0.5–1% of field); tracks pyrolysis front to <1 mm
  - Single-step vs. multi-step training: end-of-trajectory error fell from several hundred K to tens of K with multi-step (rollout) loss
- 4.3 Gas case (aw2): a **diagnosed failure mode** — **DONE (negative result)**
  - Does NOT generalize: in-sample bounded ~625 K, but **held-out diverges (3587 K)** — in-sample was optimistic (model fits its own trajectory)
  - Cause is TEMPORAL not spatial: per-cell aw2 is gentler (111 vs 221 K); surface jumps 734 K/super-step (vs aw1 41 K) under the square-pulse aero flux (298→1534 K in ~1 s)
  - Adaptive Δt-aware cadence (fine at transients, coarse on plateau) converts divergence→bounded in-sample; stay-put regularizer fixes a verified +1.5 K/step quiescent-cell bias; neither closes the held-out gap
  - Ruled out: more trajectories, gas-state-as-input, wider stencil (K=2). Solver verified sound (4-angle audit). Figures: `why_aw1_aw2.png`, `aw2_progress.png`
- 4.4 Wall-time — Fortran ~3 min vs Python ~15.6 min for `aw2_tc21` (port ~5× slower). GNN ~4× faster than Python solver on aw1 (CPU); modest — bigger super-step/mesh/amortization are the real levers (not yet exercised)
- 4.5 Mesh-resolution generalization — **`[future]`** (zero-shot fails, diagnosed; multi-res training run not done)

## 5. Discussion
- 5.1 Where the surrogate succeeds and why (mesh-native inductive bias; learns local dynamics + moving front)
- 5.2 The aw2 boundary (sharp + explainable): fixed-step rollout is the wrong vehicle for a fast localized forcing transient; in-sample-vs-held-out gap is the key honesty point → motivates the flux-form fix
- 5.3 Data selection / state-space structure lesson (trajectory vs volume sampling; the ablator's coupled T–char manifold)
- 5.4 Failure modes: rollout error accumulation (cf. bead paper), quiescent-cell bias, extrapolation beyond training envelope
- 5.5 Limitations: 1D, single material model, radiation not yet included

## 6. Future Work
- **Conservative flux-form GNN for the gas case** (predict face fluxes → divergence → uniform field gives exactly zero change, killing the quiescent bias at the root) — the most promising route to an aw2 surrogate that generalizes
- Mesh-resolution generalization (multi-res training run)
- 2D/3D mesh extension; radiation + recession physics
- Integration into the lab's design workflow
